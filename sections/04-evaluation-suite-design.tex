\section{Evaluation Suite Design}

Existing datasets and evaluation settings only partially cover the closed-loop
rescue decision chain defined in Section~3. Vision datasets cover aerial
objects, fire, and smoke. They usually focus on detection, tracking,
classification, or segmentation
\cite{xia2018dota,zhu2022visdrone,mowla2024uavsffdb,shamsoshoara2021flame,han2024msfsdb}.
They stop at visual understanding and do not evaluate downstream rescue decisions.
Some evaluation settings move beyond perception.
Wildfire VQA and disaster-oriented multi-agent benchmarks evaluate multimodal reasoning,
task allocation, path planning, or coordinated response
\cite{habibpour2026wildfirevqa,kleiner2013rmasbench}.
However, these capabilities are generally tested in isolation rather than
coupled into one rescue loop. Crisis-response benchmarks mainly focus on
text-based situational assessment and humanitarian information classification,
supporting macro-level awareness but not fine-grained UAV task execution
\cite{alam2021crisisbench}. Consequently, existing settings do not jointly test
the transition from aerial observation to fire-point detection and task-zone
construction, resource-constrained fleet planning, and runtime replanning.
Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_comparison} summarizes this comparison
across four capability dimensions.

\input{tables/benchmark-comparison}

To close this gap, we instantiate the task model of Section~3 as a controlled,
real-UAV-informed evaluation suite in AirSim. The suite evaluates whether a
system can complete the full rescue loop within a single episode, from aerial
fire-situation understanding to an executable plan and its adaptation after
runtime perturbations. Its design combines three complementary elements.

First, real-to-sim calibration constrains simulated platforms, sensing, and
mission conditions by the capabilities and safety limits of a real UAV. The
resulting scenarios are therefore physically and operationally grounded rather
than arbitrarily configured. Second, difficulty stratification varies the
complexity of fire-point layouts, fleet-resource conditions, and runtime perturbations
while preserving a common task definition. It enables controlled comparison of
models as closed-loop demands increase. Third, mission-level metrics assess the
outcome of the complete rescue process from complementary operational
perspectives, rather than collapsing performance into a single score. The
following sections detail these three elements.

\subsection{Real-to-Sim Calibration}
The first design property is realized through a co-design between the real UAV
platform and the AirSim simulation environment. As stated above, real UAV
parameters constrain every scenario. Accordingly, the real platform defines the
engineering boundary of the rescue mission, while AirSim configures controllable
and repeatable scenarios within that boundary. Rather than treating the two as
separate experimental settings, we use the real platform to define the feasible
set from which AirSim configures each simulated UAV and its environment.
For each UAV, the configured mission must satisfy
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{UAV}}=
\left\{(h,v,m,e_{\mathrm{avail}},d)\;\middle|\;
\begin{aligned}
    h &\in \mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{safe}},\quad v \in \mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{safe}},\\
    m &\leq m_{\max},\quad e_{\mathrm{req}}+e_{\mathrm{res}} \leq e_{\mathrm{avail}},\\
    d &\leq d_{\mathrm{link}}
\end{aligned}
\right\},
\label{eq:uav-envelope}
\end{equation}
where $h$, $v$, $m$, $e_{\mathrm{avail}}$, and $d$ denote flight altitude,
velocity, payload mass, available energy, and distance to the coordination link. The safe altitude
and velocity ranges are specified by the operating policy. The payload and energy
bounds are grounded in the airframe, propulsion system, and battery; the energy
reserve enforces a safe return; and the link bound is determined by the deployed
communication configuration. Thus, the numerical bounds combine hardware
specifications, flight-safety policy, and deployment configuration. Any
configuration that violates
Eq.~\ref{eq:uav-envelope} is excluded before scenario generation. The real UAV
platform is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:UAV-platform}, and its hardware
specifications are listed in Table~\ref{tab:real_uav_hardware}.

The virtual observation process is further fixed by the real camera geometry.
Under a nadir-view, locally planar-ground approximation, for altitude $h$,
horizontal field of view $\theta_{\mathrm{FOV}}$, and horizontal
resolution $N_x$, the image footprint and ground sampling distance are
\begin{equation}
W(h)=2h\tan\left(\frac{\theta_{\mathrm{FOV}}}{2}\right),\qquad
\mathrm{GSD}(h)=\frac{W(h)}{N_x}.
\label{eq:observation-geometry}
\end{equation}
Thus, the AirSim camera pose and the scale, density, and placement of fire
points are jointly constrained by what the real camera could observe, rather
than selected independently. Energy and payload limits likewise constrain route
length, task duration, and suppressible workload. Table~\ref{tab:real_to_sim_calibration}
records these calibration rules and the resulting simulation settings.

These calibrated bounds provide the common operational basis on which the
difficulty levels are constructed.

\subsection{Difficulty Stratification}
The second design property is that each evaluation episode is purpose-built
rather than randomly sampled.
Each episode instantiates the task variables defined in Section~3 through three
controlled dimensions: fleet and resource conditions, fire-point layouts, and,
where applicable, runtime perturbations. Their combinations define four stress-testing difficulty levels in
Table~\ref{tab:scenario-controls}.
Each difficulty level is instantiated by a fixed set of 20 evaluation episodes, yielding
80 episodes in total.

\input{tables/scenario-controls}

The fleet and resource conditions in every evaluation episode is constrained by the
real-to-sim calibration in Section~4.1. In particular, each simulated UAV must
satisfy the feasible operating envelope in Eq.~\ref{eq:uav-envelope}, and its
imaging, energy, payload, and communication settings follow
Table~\ref{tab:real_to_sim_calibration}. Difficulty is therefore varied only
within the engineering and operational limits established for the real platform.

The fire-point configuration of all episode is pre-specified rather than
randomly generated. These configuration controls the scene structure
and the spatial arrangement and number of fire points per task zone across
difficulty levels.
Figure~\ref{fig:difficulty-scene-examples} provides representative scenes from

the distribution of fire-point counts per task zone within each difficulty level. Together,
they illustrate the scene layouts and task-zone-level fire-point distributions
used to instantiate the four difficulty levels.

The third controlled dimension introduces runtime perturbations after mission
execution has begun. Unlike the preceding two dimensions, which specify the
initial fleet state and fire scene, these events modify the operational state
or mission constraints faced by an active plan. They are enabled only in the
hard and expert levels and are placed at predefined points in each episode so
that replanning behavior can be evaluated under repeatable conditions. Hard
episodes contain a small number of temporally separated perturbations, whereas
expert episodes contain frequent perturbations that may occur concurrently or
in succession and impose stronger communication and global scheduling
pressure. Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-perturbation-distribution} summarizes the
composition and frequency of the triggered perturbations at both levels.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
The third design property is an explicit, multi-dimensional assessment of
end-to-end behavior on the task defined in Section~3. The evaluation reports
the six mission-level metrics in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation-metrics} to retain an
interpretable view of system behavior, and combines them into an episode-level
score for comparison across methods and scenario conditions. Together, these
metrics assess whether the system completes rescue objectives promptly, uses
fleet resources responsibly, and maintains coordinated operation when
conditions change. Event stability specifically measures behavior under
runtime perturbations and is therefore reported only for the hard and expert
difficulty levels, where such perturbations are introduced. It is not
applicable to the simple and normal levels.

\input{tables/evaluation-metrics}

All six metrics are converted to normalized scores $s_k\in[0,100]$, with a
higher value consistently indicating better mission performance. Their
aggregation also accounts for the fact that episodes impose different levels
of inherent and operational complexity. Let $n_f$, $n_e$, $n_r$, and $T$
denote the number of fire points, the number of runtime perturbations, the
number of required resupply operations, and the mission duration, respectively.
For a complexity factor $x$, we use the bounded normalization
\begin{equation}
\psi(x;l,u)=
\begin{cases}
0, & x\leq l,\\
\dfrac{x-l}{u-l}, & l<x<u,\\
1, & x\geq u,
\end{cases}
\label{eq:complexity-normalization}
\end{equation}
where $l$ and $u$ are its low-pressure and saturation reference points. The
episode complexity coefficient is then defined as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho={}&
\alpha_f\psi(n_f;l_f,u_f)
+\alpha_e\psi(n_e;l_e,u_e)\\
&+\alpha_r\psi(n_r;l_r,u_r)
+\alpha_t\psi(T;l_t,u_t),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:episode-complexity}
\end{equation}
where the non-negative coefficients $\alpha_j$ sum to one and specify the
relative contribution of task scale, runtime dynamics, resource pressure, and
sustained mission demand. Because each component is bounded, $\rho\in[0,1]$
provides a common description of episode complexity across the four difficulty
levels.

The final score is computed directly by combining the six normalized mission
metrics with this complexity coefficient:
\begin{equation}
S=
\left(\sum_{k=1}^{6}w_k s_k\right)
\left(1-\lambda\rho\right),
\qquad
w_k\geq0,
\quad
\sum_{k=1}^{6}w_k=1,
\label{eq:final-evaluation-score}
\end{equation}
where $w_k$ denotes the fixed weight of each mission metric and $\lambda$
controls the maximum influence of episode complexity. The same weights,
normalization references, and complexity adjustment are used for all evaluated
methods. The component metrics remain available for diagnostic analysis, while
$S$ provides a unified measure for overall comparison.
